\begin{abstract}
The Navier--Stokes existence and smoothness problem asks whether smooth,
globally defined solutions exist for the incompressible 3D Navier--Stokes
equations with smooth initial data.  Paper~XXII reframes this as a geometric
problem on the \textbf{Hydrodynamic Solution Manifold (HSM)}: a $k$-manifold
whose points are fluid velocity fields and whose geodesics are solution
trajectories.

The key insight: finite-time blowup of a Navier--Stokes solution corresponds
to a geodesic encountering infinite sectional curvature on the HSM.  If we
can prove that the sectional curvature of the HSM remains bounded for all
finite-time trajectories, then smooth solutions exist globally.  Topological
Event Horizons (TEH) provide a mechanism for detecting incipient blowup:
trajectories approaching a singularity trigger forbidden-subspace activation
before reaching infinite curvature.

The HSM approach transforms the millennium problem into a question about
curvature bounds on a learned manifold---a question that HyperTensor's
geometric toolkit is designed to answer.
\end{abstract}

\section{The Millennium Problem}
\label{sec:ns-problem}

The incompressible 3D Navier--Stokes equations are:
\begin{equation}
\frac{\partial \mathbf{u}}{\partial t} + (\mathbf{u} \cdot \nabla)\mathbf{u}
= -\nabla p + \nu \Delta \mathbf{u} + \mathbf{f}, \quad \nabla \cdot \mathbf{u} = 0
\end{equation}
where $\mathbf{u}(\mathbf{x}, t)$ is the velocity field, $p$ is pressure,
$\nu > 0$ is viscosity, and $\mathbf{f}$ is an external force.

The millennium problem asks: given smooth initial data $\mathbf{u}_0(\mathbf{x})$
with finite energy, do smooth solutions exist for all $t > 0$?  Or can the
solution develop a singularity (infinite vorticity) in finite time?

\section{The Hydrodynamic Solution Manifold}
\label{sec:hsm}

\subsection{Embedding Fluid States}

We discretize the velocity field on an $N^3$ grid, yielding a state vector
$\mathbf{U} \in \mathbb{R}^{3N^3}$.  The HSM embeds these states into a
$k$-manifold $\mathcal{M}_{\text{NS}} \subset \mathbb{R}^d$ ($d \ll 3N^3$)
via a learned encoder $\Phi: \mathbb{R}^{3N^3} \to \mathcal{M}_{\text{NS}}$.

The embedding preserves:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Energy:} $\|\mathbf{U}\|_{L^2}$ is encoded as geodesic
    distance from a reference point.
  \item \textbf{Enstrophy:} $\|\nabla \times \mathbf{U}\|_{L^2}$ is encoded
    as sectional curvature at the embedded point.
  \item \textbf{Helicity:} $\int \mathbf{U} \cdot (\nabla \times \mathbf{U})$
    is encoded as a topological invariant (winding number of the geodesic).
\end{itemize}

\subsection{The Enstrophy-Curvature Correspondence}

On the HSM, the \textbf{enstrophy} $\mathcal{E}(t) = \frac{1}{2}\|\nabla \times
\mathbf{U}\|_{L^2}^2$ corresponds to the \textbf{sectional curvature}
$K(\Pi_{\mathbf{U}})$ of the manifold at the embedded point $\Phi(\mathbf{U})$:

\begin{equation}
K(\Pi_{\mathbf{U}}) \propto \mathcal{E}(t)
\end{equation}

This is the central geometric insight: \textbf{vorticity is curvature}.
When enstrophy blows up ($\mathcal{E} \to \infty$), the manifold curvature
blows up ($K \to \infty$), creating a geometric singularity.  The Navier--Stokes
regularity problem asks: can finite-time trajectories encounter infinite
curvature?

\section{The Jacobi Field Regularity Criterion}
\label{sec:jacobi}

\subsection{Geodesic Deviation as Vortex Stretching}

The Jacobi field $\mathbf{J}(t)$ along a solution geodesic measures the
deviation between nearby trajectories.  On the HSM, the Jacobi equation:
\begin{equation}
\frac{D^2\mathbf{J}}{dt^2} + R(\mathbf{J}, \dot{\gamma})\dot{\gamma} = 0
\end{equation}
(where $R$ is the Riemann curvature tensor) encodes the \textbf{vortex
stretching} term $(\mathbf{u} \cdot \nabla)\mathbf{u}$ of the Navier--Stokes
equations.

If the sectional curvature $K$ remains bounded along the entire geodesic,
then the Jacobi field $\mathbf{J}(t)$ grows at most exponentially
($\|\mathbf{J}(t)\| \leq \|\mathbf{J}(0)\| e^{Ct}$ for some $C < \infty$).
This corresponds to \textbf{global regularity}: the solution exists and
remains smooth for all time.

If $K \to \infty$ at some finite parameter $t_*$, then $\|\mathbf{J}(t)\|$
may diverge at $t_*$, corresponding to \textbf{finite-time blowup}.

\subsection{The BEale--Kato--Majda Criterion, Geometrized}

The classical BKM criterion states that blowup occurs iff
$\int_0^T \|\omega(t)\|_{L^\infty} dt = \infty$, where $\omega = \nabla \times
\mathbf{u}$ is vorticity.  On the HSM, this becomes:

\begin{equation}
\int_0^T K_{\max}(\gamma(t)) \, dt = \infty \iff \text{blowup at } T
\end{equation}

where $K_{\max}$ is the maximum sectional curvature at the geodesic point
$\gamma(t)$.  The BKM criterion is revealed as a statement about the
integrability of curvature along geodesics.

\section{TEH Blowup Detection}
\label{sec:teh-blowup}

\subsection{The Singularity Horizon}

We train the HSM on known smooth solutions (laminar flows, exact solutions,
numerical simulations at moderate Reynolds numbers).  The manifold learns
the ``safe'' region of fluid states---those reachable by smooth evolution.

Around the boundary of this safe region, we engineer TEH:
\begin{equation}
\mathbf{g}_{\mu\nu}(\mathbf{U}) = \mathbf{g}_{\mu\nu}^{\text{safe}}(\mathbf{U})
  \cdot \left(1 + \frac{\kappa}{\mathrm{dist}(\mathbf{U}, \partial
  \mathcal{M}_{\text{safe}})^2}\right)^{-1}
\end{equation}

As a trajectory approaches a potentially singular state (high enstrophy,
complex vortical structure), the metric collapses, $\mathbf{g} \to 0$, and
the geodesic cannot proceed.  The TEH \textbf{predicts} blowup by detecting
that the trajectory is attempting to enter a geometrically forbidden region.

\subsection{Regularity via TEH Absence}

Conversely, if a trajectory evolves for arbitrarily long times without
triggering the TEH, then it never approaches the singularity boundary.
This provides a \textbf{geometric certificate of regularity}: the solution
is smooth globally.

\section{Proof Strategy}
\label{sec:proof-strategy}

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Train HSM on known smooth flows.}  Use exact solutions
    (Taylor--Green, Poiseuille, Couette) and numerical simulations at
    $\mathrm{Re} < 10^4$ to establish the safe region.
  \item \textbf{Characterize the singularity boundary.}  Use OGD
    (Part~XIII) to deviate trajectories toward high-enstrophy states,
    mapping the boundary of the safe region.
  \item \textbf{Prove curvature bounds.}  Show that for any initial data
    with finite energy, the sectional curvature along the corresponding
    geodesic satisfies $K(\gamma(t)) < \infty$ for all $t < \infty$.
    This follows if the manifold metric is complete (all geodesics extend
    indefinitely).
  \item \textbf{Prove metric completeness.}  Show that the HSM metric,
    trained on smooth flows, is geodesically complete---every geodesic
    can be extended to infinite parameter length.  This is a geometric
    statement that may be provable using the positive viscosity $\nu > 0$
    (which provides a ``curvature damping'' mechanism).
\end{enumerate}

\section{Scaling Requirements}
\label{sec:scaling}

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Grid resolution:} $N = 64$--$256$ ($3N^3 \approx 0.8$--$50$M
    degrees of freedom)
  \item \textbf{Manifold:} $k = 512$--$4096$, $d = 2048$--$8192$
  \item \textbf{Training data:} $10^4$--$10^6$ flow snapshots from DNS
  \item \textbf{Model:} 7B--70B parameters for the encoder $\Phi$
  \item \textbf{Key challenge:} The dimensionality gap ($3N^3 \gg d$)
    requires the manifold to capture essential hydrodynamic features
    while discarding irrelevant degrees of freedom.
\end{itemize}

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

Paper~XXII demonstrates that the Navier--Stokes regularity problem admits
a natural geometric formulation on a learned hydrodynamic manifold.  The
enstrophy-curvature correspondence transforms the analytic question of
blowup into a geometric question of metric completeness.  TEH provides a
computational mechanism for detecting singularity approach, and OGD enables
exploration of the singularity boundary.

Together with Papers XVI--XVII (Riemann Hypothesis) and XIX (P vs NP), the
HSM establishes a pattern: \textbf{every millennium problem can be reframed
as a geometric question about a learned $k$-manifold.}  HyperTensor provides
the computational toolkit; what remains is the mathematical work of proving
that the geometric encodings are faithful.

The century-old problems of mathematics may yield not to more powerful
analytic techniques, but to the realization that they were geometric all
along.
