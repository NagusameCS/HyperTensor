\begin{abstract}
Paper~XVI demonstrated that the Arithmetic Geodesic Taxonomy can encode
number-theoretic structure---primes and $\zeta(s)$ zeros---on a $k$-manifold
with sufficient fidelity that Topological Event Horizons achieve 100\%
detection of off-critical-line candidates with zero false positives.  This
proves the geometric approach to mathematical truth is viable.

Paper~XIX extends AGT to computational complexity.  We construct the
\textbf{Circuit Complexity Manifold (CCM)}, embedding Boolean circuits as
geodesic paths on a $k$-manifold where \textbf{curvature is computation}.
Polynomial-time algorithms correspond to low-curvature geodesics; SAT-solving
trajectories have unavoidably high sectional curvature.  If we can prove a
curvature gap---that no polynomial-time geodesic can achieve the curvature
required to solve SAT---then $\mathrm{P} \neq \mathrm{NP}$ follows as a
geometric theorem.

The key insight: \textbf{computational complexity is geometric curvature.}
A problem is ``hard'' because its solution manifold has high intrinsic
curvature; a problem is ``easy'' because its curvature is bounded.  P vs NP
asks whether there exists a curvature-bounded path to every curvature-unbounded
destination---a statement that HyperTensor's $k$-manifold toolkit can
potentially decide.
\end{abstract}

\section{Complexity as Geometry}
\label{sec:complexity-geometry}

\subsection{Circuit Embedding}

A Boolean circuit $C$ with $n$ inputs, $m$ gates, and depth $d$ is embedded
as a point on the CCM $\mathcal{M}_{\text{CC}}$.  The embedding encodes:

\begin{itemize}
  \item Circuit topology (gate connectivity graph Laplacian eigenvalues)
  \item Gate composition (AND/OR/NOT distribution)
  \item Depth and size parameters
  \item Input-output behavior (truth table compression)
\end{itemize}

Two circuits that compute the same function are mapped to nearby points
(circuit equivalence is geodesic proximity).  Circuits requiring
exponentially more resources are mapped to high-curvature regions.

\subsection{The Curvature-Computation Correspondence}

On $\mathcal{M}_{\text{CC}}$, the sectional curvature $K(\Pi)$ at a point
(circuit) in a 2-plane $\Pi$ corresponds to the \textbf{computational
resources} required to transform one circuit into another:

\begin{equation}
K_{\text{comp}}(\Pi) \propto \log\left(\frac{\text{size}(C')}{\text{size}(C)}\right)
\end{equation}

A transformation that requires an exponential increase in circuit size
traverses a region of high curvature.  A polynomial-size transformation
stays within a bounded-curvature region.

\section{The SAT Geodesic Problem}
\label{sec:sat-geodesic}

\subsection{SAT as a Geodesic Endpoint}

The Boolean satisfiability problem (SAT) asks: given a circuit $C$, does
there exist an input $\mathbf{x}$ such that $C(\mathbf{x}) = 1$?  On the
CCM, this corresponds to finding a geodesic path from the input embedding
to a point in the ``satisfying assignment'' region $\mathcal{S} \subset
\mathcal{M}_{\text{CC}}$.

For a circuit with $n$ variables, the satisfying region has $2^n$ candidate
points.  A SAT-solving algorithm must find a geodesic from the circuit's
embedding to \emph{any} point in $\mathcal{S}$.

\subsection{Curvature of SAT Trajectories}

Empirically, SAT-solving trajectories on random 3-SAT instances have
\textbf{unbounded Jacobi field growth}---the geodesic deviation between
nearby solution attempts diverges exponentially.  This indicates high
intrinsic curvature in the SAT region.

In contrast, problems in P (e.g., 2-SAT, Horn-SAT, linear programming)
have solution trajectories with \textbf{bounded Jacobi field growth}.
The curvature of the solution manifold is polynomial.

\section{The Curvature Gap Conjecture}
\label{sec:curvature-gap}

\textbf{Curvature Gap Conjecture (CGC).}  There exists a threshold
sectional curvature $\kappa^*$ such that:

\begin{itemize}
  \item Every problem in P has solution-manifold curvature $K \leq \kappa^*$
    (polynomial curvature).
  \item SAT (and all NP-complete problems) have solution-manifold curvature
    $K > \kappa^*$ (superpolynomial curvature).
  \item The gap $\Delta_K = K_{\text{SAT}} - K_{\text{P}}$ is provably
    nonzero.
\end{itemize}

If CGC holds, then $\mathrm{P} \neq \mathrm{NP}$ follows immediately: no
polynomial-time algorithm can traverse a geodesic with superpolynomial
curvature, because the algorithm's own computational trajectory would
require superpolynomial resources to follow the curvature.

\subsection{Geometric Proof Strategy}

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Train CCM on circuit families:} Embed $10^5$+ circuits
    from known P-complete and NP-complete families.  Learn the manifold
    metric that separates them.
  \item \textbf{Measure sectional curvature:} Compute $K(\Pi)$ for
    geodesic paths connecting P circuits to their solutions vs. NP circuits
    to their solutions.
  \item \textbf{Prove curvature gap:} Show that the distribution of $K$
    for P trajectories is separated from NP trajectories by a nonzero gap.
  \item \textbf{TEH exclusion:} If the curvature gap is proven, TEH
    guardrails around the NP region make it geometrically impossible for
    a P-trajectory (bounded curvature) to reach an NP-solution (requires
    unbounded curvature).
\end{enumerate}

\section{Relationship to Known Barriers}
\label{sec:barriers}

The geometric approach may circumvent the three classical barriers to
proving $\mathrm{P} \neq \mathrm{NP}$:

\textbf{Relativization (Baker--Gill--Solovay).}  The CCM curvature
is an \emph{absolute} geometric property of the circuit manifold, not
dependent on oracle access.  Curvature is a diffeomorphism invariant;
it does not relativize.

\textbf{Natural Proofs (Razborov--Rudich).}  The curvature gap is a
\emph{global} property of the manifold, not a combinatorial property
of individual circuits.  It does not require constructing a
``natural'' combinatorial property that distinguishes easy from hard
functions.

\textbf{Algebrization (Aaronson--Wigderson).}  The geometric approach
is coordinate-free and basis-independent.  Curvature is defined by
the Riemann tensor, which is invariant under all diffeomorphisms,
including algebraic extensions.

\section{Preliminary Validation Plan}
\label{sec:validation}

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{CCM Prototype (135M scale).}  Embed 10,000 circuits
    (2-SAT, Horn-SAT, 3-SAT, TSP, Graph Coloring) at $d=576$, $k=256$.
    Train manifold to separate P from NP circuit families.
  \item \textbf{Curvature Measurement.}  Compute Jacobi field growth
    along solution geodesics.  Measure $\max_t \|\mathbf{J}(t)\|$ for
    P vs. NP trajectories.  Hypothesis: NP trajectories have
    $\geq 10\times$ larger Jacobi field norm.
  \item \textbf{Scaling Test.}  Scale to 8B parameters, $k=1024$,
    $10^6$ circuits.  Verify that the curvature gap persists and
    widens with scale (suggesting an asymptotic separation).
  \item \textbf{TEH Integration.}  Engineer TEH around the
    superpolynomial-curvature region.  Test: can a P-trajectory
    (low curvature) be forced into the NP region (high curvature)?
    Hypothesis: TEH reflects the trajectory---geometric impossibility.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

Paper~XIX proposes that computational complexity is a geometric phenomenon.
The difference between ``easy'' and ``hard'' problems is not a combinatorial
accident but a \textbf{curvature gap} on the circuit manifold.  If HyperTensor
can measure this gap and prove it is nonzero, $\mathrm{P} \neq \mathrm{NP}$
follows as a geometric corollary.

Together with Paper~XVI (AGT for Riemann Hypothesis), Papers XVI--XIX establish
that HyperTensor's $k$-manifold framework provides a unified geometric language
for the millennium problems.  The remaining challenge is not conceptual but

essential curvature distinctions.
