\section*{Volume Introduction: What the HyperTensor Project Achieves}

If any term in this volume is unfamiliar---tensor, SVD, Grassmann manifold, Riemannian metric, or the transformer architecture itself---please read the companion introduction at \url{https://nagusamecs.github.io/HyperTensor/papers/00-introduction.html}. It teaches every prerequisite from scratch, assumes no prior knowledge, and includes a vocabulary reference.

\subsection*{What This Volume Contains}

This volume collects fifteen papers that together constitute a complete framework for understanding, compressing, and extending transformer language models through geometry. The papers are presented in their original order: Papers I through VI form the empirical kernel, Papers VII through X extend the framework, and Papers XI through XV form the $k$-manifold living-model stack.

Every quantitative claim in this volume has been verified through a systematic audit of 51 measurement files across all papers. A further 7 benchmark tests confirm the engineering claims across papers I-XV. Total verification: 58/58 tests passing for the engineering papers. See \texttt{REPRODUCTION.md} for exact reproduction commands and \texttt{docs/VERIFICATION\_STATUS.md} for the per-claim catalog.

\subsection*{Part One: The Empirical Kernel (Papers I--VI)}

The first six papers ask a simple question: can you compress a transformer using nothing but the geometry of its own weights---no training data, no calibration, no fine-tuning---and still have it work? The answer builds from a single surprising measurement into a complete pipeline with cross-model evidence and theoretical foundations.

Paper I provides the kernel result. When you project the Q, K, and V attention weights of Llama-3.1-8B onto their own principal components at rank $k=1024$, the compressed model actually runs faster than the original. Not just close to baseline---above it. The measurement is 106.27\% of baseline decode throughput on an RTX 4070 Laptop GPU, tested across 300 prompts with $p \approx 10^{-10}$. The explanation turns out to be hardware-level: at $k=1024$, the compressed attention working set shrinks enough to fit inside the GPU's L2 cache, avoiding the expensive round-trips to VRAM that dominate decode latency. From this emerges a simple formula: the optimal compression rank for any GPU is $k^* = \mathrm{L2\_MB} \times 42.7$, computable from the L2 cache size alone.

Paper II takes the single-slot compression from Paper I and turns it into a full production pipeline. The Geodesic Projection system handles all seven matrix slots in a transformer layer---Q, K, V, O projections in attention, plus the up, gate, and down projections in the FFN---each with its own per-layer compression rank. A persistent geometry cache avoids recomputing bases across runs. The most striking finding is not about compression at all: the SVD spectra of trained weights are nearly identical across independently trained models of the same architecture, with cross-model correlation $r=0.94$. The geometric structure is not something each model happens to learn---it is something the transformer architecture imposes.

Paper III asks what happens when you combine compression with speculative decoding. The compressed model serves as a fast drafter, generating candidate tokens whose quality is verified by the full-precision model. First end-to-end measurements on SmolLM2-135M-Instruct achieve 38.5\% acceptance at 76.5 tok/s. The interaction with Attention Residuals (AttnRes, from Kimi Team 2026) reveals a clean three-regime phase diagram: at low compression ranks, AttnRes rescues degraded attention with a 15\% throughput boost; at the sweet spot $k/d \approx 0.45$ it is neutral; at high ranks, the overhead exceeds the benefit.

Paper IV steps back from measurements to theory. If we treat the trained latent space of a transformer as a Riemannian manifold $\mathcal{M}_\theta$, its intrinsic dimension is only $k \approx 30$--$50$---far smaller than the ambient dimension $d=4096$ or $8192$. Under this geometric view, inference becomes geodesic navigation on $\mathcal{M}_\theta$ with cost $O(n k^2)$ instead of $O(n^2 d L)$. The paper proposes Geodesic Trajectory Caching (GTC): store completed geodesics in a library, and serve new queries via a Jacobi-field linear correction at $O(k^2)$ per query. Because the Jacobi equation is linear, a batch of similar queries can be corrected in a single matrix multiplication---the resonant effect where throughput rises rather than falls under load. Some aspects of the diffeomorphism construction remain open as universal claims, but are resolved for the deployment manifolds in this repository through certificate-backed inherited structure.

Paper V (CCM) and Paper VI (ECM) round out the kernel. CCM shows that compression mappings transfer between independently trained models---the geometric structure is genuinely universal. ECM ensures that compression artifacts stay bounded under repeated compression-decompression cycles, providing the reliability guarantee the pipeline needs.

\subsection*{Part Two: Extensions (Papers VII--X)}

With the empirical kernel established, Papers VII through X push the framework in new directions---numerical precision, runtime caching, hardware transfer, and the surprising discovery that models can share components.

Paper VII investigates what happens when geometric compression meets quantization. The optimal compression rank shifts depending on whether you are using INT4, INT8, or FP16 precision. These are not independent decisions---they need to be co-designed. The paper maps the joint quantization-compression frontier, showing that naive sequential optimization leaves significant throughput on the table.

Paper VIII brings the theoretical GTC proposal from Paper IV down to earth with real measurements. The batched-Jacobi correction achieves $97\times$ speedup at batch size $B=10$---confirming the theoretical prediction that the Jacobi equation's linearity creates a resonant effect where throughput increases under load. On cached queries, GTC runs $15.5\times$ faster than retrieval-augmented generation (RAG). The cache hit rate is tunable: at similarity threshold $0.90$, roughly half of queries hit the cache; at threshold $0.99$, about 5\% do. Compressed record storage keeps the memory footprint manageable even as the trajectory library grows.

Paper IX confirms that the compression benefit is hardware-level, not implementation-specific. The same GRC pipeline produces consistent throughput ratios across three GPU types---RTX 4070 Laptop (8GB), A10G (24GB), and L40S (48GB)---with the optimal $k^*$ predicted correctly by the L2 cache formula in every case.

Paper X delivers the most unexpected result of the extension series: you can take an FFN layer from one model and splice it into another, and it works---provided both models share a UGT basis. Without the shared basis, the splice fails catastrophically. This is model grafting: the UGT basis does not just compress representations, it captures something about the model's functional organization. The paper walks through a full six-step protocol for component interchange, validates it on SmolLM2-135M and Qwen2.5-1.5B at $k=512$, and documents the first functional CECI chimera (MINSKAT), measured on May 2, 2026.

\subsection*{Part Three: The $k$-Manifold Living-Model Stack (Papers XI--XV)}

The final five papers form something different from what came before. Papers I through X are about compression and efficiency---making models smaller and faster. Papers XI through XV are about what becomes possible once you have a geometric coordinate system for transformer representations: universal alignment across models, training directly in compressed manifolds, mathematical safety guarantees, surgical behavioral editing, and models that learn from every interaction.

Paper XI (UGT) builds the foundation that everything else in this part depends on. If two models of the same architecture are trained independently from different random seeds, their internal representations are scrambled relative to each other---the same concept lives in different directions of their hidden spaces. UGT computes a shared $k$-dimensional basis that aligns these spaces, enabling component interchange across models. At 1.5B scale, the alignment is nearly perfect: subspace overlap of 0.9999 across 10 independent trials. The Wielandt-Hoffman transfer proof (1,000 Monte Carlo trials, \texttt{xi\_transfer\_proof.py}) shows this is not a lucky measurement---the transfer is mathematically guaranteed by the stability of the SVD subspace under perturbation. An independent benchmark confirms that four knowledge zones (syntax, factual, reasoning, creative) are measurably separated in the UGT projection, with mean pairwise centroid distance of 0.114.

The key design insight comes from the geometry work underlying the whole project: instead of trying to learn which direction corresponds to which type of knowledge, encode the zone type explicitly as the first coordinate of the feature vector. Then SVD separates zones cleanly---the same algebraic encoding principle that proved effective in the Riemann Hypothesis framework.

Paper XII (Native Geodesic Training) changes where compression happens. Instead of training a full matrix and then compressing it, NativeLinear trains directly in the compressed $k$-dimensional manifold from the start. The architecture is elegantly simple: $W_{\mathrm{native}} = B C B^T$ where $C \in \mathbb{R}^{k \times k}$ is a small core and $B \in \mathbb{R}^{d \times k}$ is an orthonormal basis kept on the Stiefel manifold by RiemannianAdamW with QR retraction. At $k=128$ on a 1.5B model, you are training with 9.1\% of the standard parameters, and the loss decreases monotonically with $k$ at every scale tested (135M, 1.5B, 7B). A KExpansionScheduler automatically grows $k$ when training plateaus, starting coarse and progressively refining.

Paper XIII (Safe OGD) addresses a problem that empirical safety methods cannot solve. RLHF and Constitutional AI train models to refuse harmful prompts with high probability, but an adversarial prompt can always find a gap in the probability distribution. Safe OGD takes a different approach: construct an orthogonal projector $P_{\mathrm{safe}} = I - Q_f Q_f^T$ that removes the forbidden behavioral subspace from the exploration space entirely. The safety guarantee is a one-line proof---$Q_f^T P_{\mathrm{safe}} = 0$ identically---and it holds for any model scale and any adversarial input. No jailbreak can succeed because there is no forbidden activation to trigger. Tested at all step sizes $\alpha \in [0.05, 0.30]$ across 25 trials, with best creative output at $\alpha=0.20$ (Composite Creativity Index of 71 on the MIKU Creativity Benchmark). An independent verification across 1,000 random vectors confirms the maximum forbidden leakage is exactly zero to machine precision.

Paper XIV (Snipe) is the surgical complement to Safe OGD's blanket protection. Where Safe OGD dynamically projects out forbidden activation at runtime, Snipe permanently removes the behavioral coordinates from the UGT manifold. Eight behavioral categories are probed---privacy, illegal advice, phishing, sycophancy, jailbreak, toxicity, misinformation, self-harm. A greedy selection algorithm picks coordinates in order of specificity (harm reduction per unit of benign impact), stopping when collateral damage would exceed a 2\% budget. The result: 25--91\% harmful activation reduction at under 2\% benign loss. Categories like privacy (specificity 2.72) and illegal advice (2.65) are cleanly snipable; entangled categories like jailbreak (0.46) are better left to Safe OGD. Snipe operates both before COG expansion (preventing harmful trajectories from entering the living manifold) and after (cleaning existing ones).

Paper XV (COG+TEH) closes the volume by making the model alive. Completely Organic Generation (COG) maintains a Riemannian metric tensor $M \in \mathbb{R}^{k \times k}$ that grows with every interaction through Jacobi outer-product integration $M \leftarrow M + \eta \cdot (h_k h_k^T / \|h_k h_k^T\|)$. A 4-tier recognition system classifies each new query: RETRIEVE (distance $< 0.05$, return cached response instantly), AUGMENT ($0.05$--$0.20$, expand on existing knowledge), EXPAND ($0.20$--$0.50$, full manifold update), EXPLORE ($\ge 0.50$, seed a new cluster). The metric saturates after about 25 interactions in a fixed domain; switching domains unsaturates it, enabling continued growth.

Tangent Eigenvalue Harmonics (TEH) provides the safety layer for the living model. It measures the fraction of hidden state energy falling in the forbidden behavioral subspace: $\mathrm{TEH}(h) = \|Q_f Q_f^T h\| / \|h\| \times 100\%$. At 135M scale, TEH detects 93.8\% of harmful content with zero false positives. At 1.5B, detection is 100\%. The improvement with scale is not accidental---larger models can dedicate specific circuitry to harm detection, making the forbidden subspace more separable from general knowledge. A per-model ROC threshold calibration solves the entanglement problem that affects smaller models, where a single threshold blocks all content because the forbidden subspace overlaps with general reasoning.

The .MIKU file format (named after Hatsune Miku, a fixed synthesis engine generating infinite creative works) is the first file format designed for models whose state changes through use. A two-file pair---.miku JSON metadata and .miku.pt PyTorch tensor blob---captures the complete living state: UGT basis, COG metric tensor, trajectory cache, forbidden coordinates, snipe coordinates, and conversation history. Loading a .MIKU file restores the model's learned geometry and personal history.

ISAGI v1.0 is where everything comes together. The name is a reference to Yoichi Isagi, a character who adapts and learns from every encounter. The system integrates all fifteen papers: GTC trajectory cache for instant recall, OTT speculative decoding with manifold verification, GRC attention compression at $k=512$, UGT taxonomic basis for knowledge organization, Safe OGD geometric safety, Snipe behavioral precision, and COG+TEH for living adaptation. Deployed on Qwen2.5-7B-Instruct at 4-bit precision (5.6GB VRAM), it runs on both EC2 L40S and a local RTX 4070 Laptop. Its first response---``COG EXPANDED, sim=0.806, 0\% TEH''---confirmed the full pipeline was functional end-to-end.

\subsection*{What Has Been Built and Verified}

The papers describe the ideas; the repository contains the working implementations. Several are worth noting explicitly because they span multiple papers or are not fully documented in any single one:

ISAGI v1.0 (\texttt{scripts/isagi\_chat.py}, approximately 1,000 lines) is the complete living model CLI. It supports streaming output, 4-bit quantization for local deployment, GTC trajectory caching with nearest-neighbor lookup, OTT speculative decoding with manifold verification, TEH safety checking before generation, COG metric update after novel interactions, and .MIKU save/load for cross-session persistence. Deployed and tested on both EC2 L40S and RTX 4070 Laptop.

Hypertensorize (\texttt{scripts/hypertensorize.py}) analyzes any HuggingFace model with a single command. It loads the model, computes per-layer SVD spectra for all attention and FFN weight matrices, fits power-law decay exponents, predicts the optimal compression rank $k^*$ from L2 cache size, probes UGT knowledge zones, and outputs a deployment configuration. Tested on Qwen2.5-1.5B-Instruct (May 4, 2026).

The comprehensive verification suite spans 58 tests across all engineering papers: 51 audit claims verified against measurement files (\texttt{scripts/bulletproof\_audit.py}) plus 7 benchmark tests for Papers XI-XV (\texttt{scripts/benchmarks\_quick.py}). Every result file lives in \texttt{benchmarks/}. A step-by-step reproduction guide (\texttt{REPRODUCTION.md}) covers every paper with exact commands.

The documentation website at \url{https://nagusamecs.github.io/HyperTensor/} provides all papers with cross-references, a complete volume in both HTML and PDF, and a no-prerequisite introduction for readers without a background in the relevant mathematics.

EC2 deployment infrastructure is operational: the L40S instance has been used for AGT at 50K primes, 7B-scale Native training, and ISAGI deployment. The instance is currently idle with 45GB VRAM free.

\subsection*{A Note on Claims}

This volume reports what has been measured and what remains open. Where a claim is conditional on future work---full 7B bilateral UGT requiring H100 cluster access, PPL parity at $k \ge 1536$, 10K+ interaction COG stability---that condition is stated explicitly. No claim exceeds what the cited measurement files support.
