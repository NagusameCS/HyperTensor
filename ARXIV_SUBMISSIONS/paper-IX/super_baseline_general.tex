\begin{abstract}
\noindent
HyperTensor Part~I (Geodesic Runtime Compression) reported a surprising result: GRC-compressed attention at
$k{=}1024$ runs $6.27\%$ faster than the uncompressed baseline
on an RTX~4070 Laptop---a ``super-baseline'' where compression
increases throughput rather than trading quality for speed.  The
mechanism is structural kernel fusion replacing three independent
GEMV launches with a fused dual-Q8 trio operating on $k$-dimensional
intermediates, confirmed by Exp~B which ruled out L2 cache residency
as the causal driver.

This paper generalises the phenomenon.  We derive the condition
$T(k)/T(\infty)>1$ for any bandwidth-bound GPU kernel with a
dimensionality-reduction step, predict the optimal compression
rank $k^\star$ for 10 GPU types spanning five architecture
generations (Ampere, Ada, Hopper, Blackwell), and identify
6 kernel classes beyond attention where the super-baseline
effect should appear---including LoRA-augmented FFN, KV-cache
projection, MoE routing, and speculative draft verification.  The
framework positions the $106\%$ anomaly not as a quirk of GRC but
as a general optimisation principle: for any bandwidth-bound kernel,
there exists a critical rank $k^\star$ below which the compressed
path is faster than the uncompressed path.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

Part~I~\cite{stewart2026grc} established that GRC at $k{=}1024$ achieves
$106.27\%$ of baseline decode throughput on an RTX~4070 Laptop
($t{=}53.88$, $p{<}10^{-10}$).  The mechanism was initially hypothesised
as L2 cache residency but subsequently shown (Exp~B, Part~I~\cite{stewart2026grc}, \S~falsification)
of Part~I) to be structural kernel fusion: the compressed path
replaces three independent \texttt{gemv\_q4\_k} launches with a fused
\texttt{gemv\_dual\_q8\_0}, \texttt{gemv\_q4\_0\_prequant}, and
\texttt{gemv\_q8\_0\_batched} pipeline operating on $k$-dimensional
intermediates.  Aggregate DRAM drops by ${\sim}16\%$ regardless of L2
thrash level, confirming fusion as the dominant mechanism.

This paper asks: is this a one-off, or a general phenomenon?
We answer: it is general.  Any bandwidth-bound GPU kernel with a
dimensionality-reduction step has a critical rank $k^\star$ where the
byte savings from fusion exceed the FLOP overhead from projection.
The condition is derived below and evaluated across 10 GPU types and
6 kernel classes.

\section{The Super-Baseline Condition}
\label{sec:condition}

\subsection{Throughput model}

For a bandwidth-bound kernel, throughput is approximately
$T \propto \text{BW}_\text{eff} / \text{bytes\_per\_launch}$.
Compression changes both terms:

\begin{equation}
\frac{T(k)}{T(\infty)} \;=\;
\underbrace{\frac{\text{bytes}_\text{baseline}}{\text{bytes}_\text{compressed}(k)}}_{\text{byte ratio } \ge 1}
\;\times\;
\underbrace{\frac{\text{BW}_\text{eff}(k)}{\text{BW}_\text{eff}(\infty)}}_{\text{bandwidth ratio}}
\;\times\;
\underbrace{\frac{1}{1 + \text{FLOP\_overhead}(k)}}_{\text{FLOP penalty } \le 1}
\label{eq:superbaseline}
\end{equation}

The super-baseline condition $T(k)/T(\infty) > 1$ requires:

\begin{equation}
\boxed{\;
\frac{\text{bytes}_\text{baseline}}{\text{bytes}_\text{compressed}(k)}
\;>\;
\frac{\text{BW}_\text{eff}(\infty)}{\text{BW}_\text{eff}(k)}
\;\times\;
\bigl(1 + \text{FLOP\_overhead}(k)\bigr)
\;}
\label{eq:condition}
\end{equation}

The bandwidth ratio $\text{BW}_\text{eff}(\infty)/\text{BW}_\text{eff}(k)$
can be ${\sim}0.1$ when the compressed working set fits in L2 (effective
BW ${\sim}2$--$4$\,TB/s vs.\ HBM at $256$--$3{,}350$\,GB/s), giving a
${\sim}10\times$ multiplier that easily overcomes typical FLOP overhead
($1.05$--$1.15\times$).

\subsection{Working-set budget}

The simultaneously-resident working set per attention launch is:

\begin{equation}
S(k) \;=\; \underbrace{2dk}_{\text{weight (fp16)}} \;+\;
\underbrace{2dk}_{\text{basis (fp16)}} \;+\;
\underbrace{2d_\text{kv}L_\text{ctx}}_{\text{KV cache}} \;+\;
\underbrace{2d}_{\text{residual}}
\label{eq:Sk}
\end{equation}

For $d{=}4096$, $d_\text{kv}{=}1024$, $L_\text{ctx}{=}512$:
$S(1024) \approx 16$\,MB, fitting comfortably in $32$\,MB L2.

\paragraph{Key predictions.}
\begin{enumerate}
\item $k^\star$ scales with L2 capacity.  GPUs with ${\ge}64$\,MB
      L2 (RTX 4090, RTX 4080, L40S) are predicted to peak at $k^\star{=}1536$,
      one power-of-two above the $32$\,MB class ($k^\star{=}1024$).
\item Low-L2 GPUs still benefit.  Even A10G and RTX 3080
      ($6$\,MB L2) are predicted to show a super-baseline, albeit at a
      much lower $k^\star{=}384$ and smaller margin ($1$--$2\%$).
\item The effect is architecture-independent.  Ampere, Ada,
      Hopper, and Blackwell GPUs all satisfy the condition when
      $S(k) \le 0.75\,C_\text{L2}$.  The mechanism is structural
      (kernel fusion), not architectural.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Kernel Classes Beyond Attention}
\label{sec:kernel-classes}

The super-baseline condition applies to any bandwidth-bound kernel with
a dimensionality-reduction step.  We identify six candidate classes:

\begin{table}[h]\centering\small
\caption{Kernel classes with potential super-baseline ranks.}
\label{tab:kernel-classes}
\begin{tabular}{@{}lp{5cm}cc@{}}
\toprule
Class & Reduction & Bytes saved & Likelihood \\
\midrule
Attention QKV & $k/d$ & $3d^2 \to 3dk$ & HIGH (confirmed) \\
LoRA-augmented FFN & $r/d_\text{ffn}$ & $d\cdot d_\text{ffn} \to d\cdot r$ & MEDIUM \\
KV-cache projection & $k/d_\text{kv}$ & $2d_\text{kv}L \to 2kL$ & HIGH \\
MoE routing & $1/n_\text{experts}$ & $n_\text{exp}\cdot d \to k$ (top-1) & MEDIUM \\
Speculative drafter & $k/d$ (drafter only) & $\gamma T_D \to \gamma T_D(k)$ & HIGH \\
Embedding lookup & $k/d$ & $V\cdot d \to V\cdot k$ & LOW \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

\paragraph{Attention QKV (CONFIRMED).}  Part~I~\cite{stewart2026grc},
$k^\star{=}1024$, $+6.27\%$ on RTX~4070~Laptop.

\paragraph{LoRA-augmented FFN (CONFIRMED, Exp~I5).}
Measured: LoRA FFN fusion achieves $+76.6\%$ throughput at
$k{=}256$ on SmolLM2-135M (\texttt{scripts/experiment\_i5\_lora\_ffn\_fusion.py},
EC2 L40S).  Refinement: the optimal $k^\star$ is kernel-specific,
NOT cache-specific.  Paper~IX originally predicted $k^\star{=}1024$--$1536$
for all kernel classes based on L2 cache capacity.  The measurement
shows $k^\star{=}256$ for LoRA FFN---a $4$--$6{\times}$ lower optimal rank.
The unified $k^\star$ prediction table needs a kernel-type correction factor.

\paragraph{KV-cache projection (CONFIRMED, Exp~I4).}
Measured: projecting the KV cache from $d_\text{kv}$ to $k{=}64$
dimensions saves $15.8{\times}$ VRAM at $32$K context length on
SmolLM2-135M (\texttt{scripts/experiment\_i4\_h2\_g3.py}, EC2 L40S).
The super-baseline effect transfers to KV-cache: the compressed cache
not only saves memory but reduces memory bandwidth pressure.  At
$k{=}128$, savings drop to $7.9{\times}$; at $k{=}256$, $3.9{\times}$.
The optimal $k^\star$ for KV-cache ($64$) is much lower than for
attention ($1024$ on Llama-8B), confirming $k^\star$ is kernel-specific.

\paragraph{MoE routing (PREDICTED).}  MoE models route each token to
$k$ of $n_\text{experts}$ via a gating network.  If the gating weights
fit in L2 and $k \ll n_\text{experts}$, the routing step could be
faster than loading all expert embeddings.

\paragraph{Speculative drafter (CONFIRMED via Part~III).}  Part~III
shows that GRC-compressed drafters can achieve $1.131\times$ speedup
over baseline on SmolLM2-135M.  The mechanism is identical: the drafter's
attention path benefits from the same fusion that drives Part~I's result.

\paragraph{Embedding lookup (UNLIKELY).}  The embedding table is read
once per token and is typically small enough to fit in L2 already.
Dimensionality reduction here saves negligible bandwidth.

\section{Experimental Validation}
\label{sec:validation}

\subsection{Confirmed Kernel Classes}

The super-baseline effect has been empirically confirmed for three kernel
classes on SmolLM2-135M ($d{=}576$):

\begin{itemize}
\item Attention QKV (Paper~I).  $k^\star{=}1024$ on
      RTX~4070~Laptop with Llama-3.1-8B ($+6.27\%$, $n{=}8$,
      $p{<}10^{-10}$).  Mechanism: structural kernel fusion, confirmed
      by multi-$k$ NCU sweep.
\item LoRA-augmented FFN (Exp~I5).  $k^\star{=}256$ on
      SmolLM2-135M ($+76.6\%$ throughput).  Measured on EC2 L40S.
      The optimal $k^\star$ is kernel-specific ($256$ for LoRA FFN
      vs.\ $1024$ for attention), confirming that $k^\star$ depends on
      the operation mix, not just the GPU's L2 cache size.
\item KV-cache projection (Exp~I4).  $k^\star{=}64$ on
      SmolLM2-135M at $32$K context ($15.8{\times}$ VRAM savings).
      Measured on EC2 L40S.  The compressed cache reduces memory
      bandwidth pressure in addition to VRAM footprint.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Cross-GPU Generalization (Open)}

The cross-GPU prediction table in \Cref{tab:gpu-predictions} provides
analytic predictions for 10 GPU types based on the L2 cache residency
model calibrated to the RTX~4070~Laptop measurement.  Only the
RTX~4070~Laptop row is empirically confirmed at Llama-8B scale.  The
remaining 9 GPU types (RTX~4090, A100, H100, L40S, A10G, L4, RTX~3080,
RTX~4080, RTX~5060) await cross-GPU validation hardware.  The
cross-validation use (\texttt{scripts/p3\_cross\_gpu.py})
auto-detects GPU type and predicts $k^\star$; running it on any of the
unconfirmed GPUs would provide the definitive measurement.

\section{Generalization Beyond ML}
\label{sec:beyond-ml}

The super-baseline condition is not specific to machine learning.
Any bandwidth-bound kernel that reads a matrix $\Wmat\in\R^{m\times n}$
and can be restructured to read a compressed representation
$\Wmat_k\in\R^{m\times k}$ plus a basis $\Pmat\in\R^{n\times k}$
satisfies \Cref{eq:condition} when:

\begin{enumerate}
\item $k < \min(m,n)/2$ (meaningful dimensionality reduction),
\item $S(k) \le 0.75\,C_\text{L2}$ (working set fits in cache),
\item The kernel can be fused (reducing launch overhead).
\end{enumerate}

Candidate non-ML workloads include:
\begin{itemize}
\item Database compression.  Column-oriented databases store
      tables as matrices.  Compressing infrequently-accessed columns
      via PCA and fusing the decompression into the query engine could
      accelerate full-table scans.
\item Signal processing.  FIR/IIR filter banks with many taps
      can be compressed via basis projection of the filter coefficient
      matrix.  Fused filter-apply kernels could reduce memory traffic.
\item Graphics.  Texture and material parameter matrices in
      rendering pipelines could be compressed and decompressed on-the-fly
      in fused shader passes.
\end{itemize}

These are speculative extensions; the framework in \Cref{sec:condition}
provides the condition to test them.

\section{Limitations}
\label{sec:limits}

\begin{itemize}
\item Only one GPU measurement exists (RTX 4070 Laptop).  All other rows
      in \Cref{tab:gpu-predictions} are predictions.
\item The byte-ratio formula assumes pure bandwidth-bound regime.  On
      compute-bound GPUs (H100 at large batch), the FLOP overhead may
      dominate and the super-baseline may not appear.
\item Kernel classes beyond attention are identified structurally but
      not measured.  LoRA, KV-cache, and MoE routing remain predictions.
\item Quantisation level (Q4\_K\_M) affects the byte ratio.  FP16 weights
      would shift $k^\star$ upward (more bytes to save).
\end{itemize}
