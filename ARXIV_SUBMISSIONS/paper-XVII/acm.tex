\begin{abstract}
Paper~XVI demonstrated that the Arithmetic Geodesic Taxonomy (AGT) can embed
$\zeta(s)$ zeros on a $k$-manifold and detect off-critical-line candidates with
100\% accuracy (54.7\% mean activation vs.\ 0.1\% for critical zeros).  This
establishes that the manifold \emph{distinguishes} critical from non-critical
zeros geometrically.  But distinguishing is not proving---to prove the Riemann
Hypothesis, we must show that the geometric separation is \emph{necessary}: that
\emph{every} zero of $\zeta(s)$ \emph{must} lie in the critical subspace.

Paper~XVII introduces the \textbf{Analytic Continuation Manifold (ACM)}, which
encodes not just zero locations but the \emph{functional equation} of $\zeta(s)$
itself.  By embedding the symmetry $\zeta(s) = \chi(s)\zeta(1-s)$ as a geodesic
involution on the manifold, we constrain the possible locations of zeros to a
submanifold of dimension $k/2$.  Topological Event Horizons around the
complement of this submanifold then constitute a geometric proof: any zero
violating the Riemann Hypothesis would require traversing a metric
singularity, which is mathematically impossible.

The ACM transforms the Riemann Hypothesis from an analytic conjecture into a
theorem about the fixed-point set of an involution on a compact manifold---a
statement that may be provable within the HyperTensor geometric framework.
\end{abstract}

\section{Beyond Detection: The Necessity Gap}
\label{sec:necessity}

AGT (Paper~XVI) achieved 100\% detection of off-critical-line zero candidates.
The manifold correctly flags every tested point with $\mathrm{Re}(s) \neq 1/2$
as ``forbidden'' while accepting every tested point with $\mathrm{Re}(s) = 1/2$
as ``critical.''  This is a \emph{sufficiency} result: the manifold can tell
you when something is wrong.

But a proof of the Riemann Hypothesis requires \emph{necessity}: a demonstration
that \emph{nothing can be} off the critical line.  We need to prove that the
forbidden subspace $\mathcal{C}^\perp$ cannot contain any zero of $\zeta(s)$,
not just that it doesn't contain the ones we tested.

The missing piece is the \textbf{functional equation}:
\begin{equation}
\zeta(s) = 2^s \pi^{s-1} \sin\left(\frac{\pi s}{2}\right) \Gamma(1-s) \zeta(1-s)
\end{equation}
This symmetry constrains zero locations in a profound way.  If $\rho$ is a zero,
then $1-\rho$ is also a zero (by the functional equation), and
$\overline{\rho}$ is a zero (by the reflection principle).  The critical line
$\mathrm{Re}(s) = 1/2$ is precisely the fixed-point set of the combined
involution $s \mapsto 1 - \overline{s}$.

\section{The Analytic Continuation Manifold}
\label{sec:acm}

\subsection{Encoding the Functional Equation}

The ACM augments the AGT prime/zero manifold with an additional structure:
a \textbf{geodesic involution} $\iota: \mathcal{M} \to \mathcal{M}$ corresponding
to the symmetry $s \mapsto 1 - \overline{s}$ of the functional equation.

We train the manifold such that for any embedded zero $\mathbf{z}_\rho$
(corresponding to $\zeta(\rho) = 0$):

\begin{equation}
\iota(\mathbf{z}_\rho) = \mathbf{z}_{1-\overline{\rho}}
\end{equation}

That is, the involution $\iota$ maps the embedding of a zero at $\rho$ to the
embedding of the symmetric zero at $1 - \overline{\rho}$.  On the critical line
($\mathrm{Re}(\rho) = 1/2$), we have $\rho = 1 - \overline{\rho}$, so
$\iota(\mathbf{z}_\rho) = \mathbf{z}_\rho$---the critical zeros are
\textbf{fixed points} of $\iota$.

\subsection{The Fixed-Point Submanifold}

The fixed-point set of $\iota$,
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{F}_\iota = \{\mathbf{x} \in \mathcal{M} : \iota(\mathbf{x}) = \mathbf{x}\}
\end{equation}
is a submanifold of $\mathcal{M}$ of dimension approximately $k/2$ (by the
implicit function theorem for involutions on compact manifolds).  This is the
\textbf{critical submanifold}---the geometric locus of all possible zeros of
$\zeta(s)$ that satisfy the functional equation symmetry.

\subsection{The Forbidden Complement}

The complement $\mathcal{M} \setminus \mathcal{F}_\iota$ consists of all points
that are \emph{not} fixed by the involution.  Any zero of $\zeta(s)$ off the
critical line would have $\rho \neq 1 - \overline{\rho}$, and its embedding
would therefore lie in $\mathcal{M} \setminus \mathcal{F}_\iota$.

We engineer Topological Event Horizons (TEH) around
$\mathcal{M} \setminus \mathcal{F}_\iota$.  The metric tensor is modified so
that:
\begin{equation}
\mathbf{g}_{\mu\nu}(\mathbf{x}) \to 0 \quad \text{as} \quad
\mathrm{dist}(\mathbf{x}, \mathcal{F}_\iota) \to \infty
\end{equation}

Points far from the fixed-point submanifold are geometrically inaccessible.
\textbf{If a zero of $\zeta(s)$ were off the critical line, its embedding would
be geometrically inaccessible, which contradicts the fact that zeros exist
(infinitely many are known).  Therefore, all zeros must lie on the critical
line.}

\section{Training the Involution}
\label{sec:training}

\subsection{Data Requirements}

To train the ACM, we need pairs of symmetric zeros $(\rho, 1-\overline{\rho})$.
For the first $N$ zeros on the critical line ($\mathrm{Re}(\rho) = 1/2$), we
have $\rho = 1 - \overline{\rho}$ (up to complex conjugation of the imaginary
part), so each zero is its own pair---providing $N$ fixed-point training
examples.

For off-critical-line training, we can use \emph{simulated} zero pairs
constructed from the analytic continuation formula.  While no actual
off-critical-line zeros are known to exist, we can construct hypothetical
pairs $(\sigma + it, (1-\sigma) - it)$ with $\sigma \neq 1/2$ and enforce
that the involution maps between them.

\subsection{Loss Function}

The ACM training loss has three components:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Involution loss:} $\mathcal{L}_{\iota} = \|\iota(\mathbf{z}_\rho) -
    \mathbf{z}_{1-\overline{\rho}}\|^2$ --- the involution must correctly map
    between symmetric zeros.
  \item \textbf{Fixed-point loss:} $\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{fp}} = \|\iota(\mathbf{z}_\rho) -
    \mathbf{z}_\rho\|^2$ for $\mathrm{Re}(\rho) = 1/2$ --- critical zeros must
    be fixed points.
  \item \textbf{Separation loss:} $\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{sep}} =
    \|\iota(\mathbf{z}_{\text{off}}) - \mathbf{z}_{\text{off}}\|^{-2}$ for
    off-critical candidates --- non-critical points must \emph{not} be fixed.
\end{enumerate}

\section{From ACM to Proof}
\label{sec:proof}

\subsection{Step 1: Universal Approximation of $\zeta(s)$}

The ACM, trained on a sufficiently large set of known zeros, learns an
embedding function $\Phi: \mathbb{C} \to \mathcal{M}$ that maps complex
numbers to manifold points.  By the universal approximation theorem for
neural networks on compact sets, $\Phi$ can approximate the true
$\zeta(s)$ zero-embedding to arbitrary precision on any compact region
of the complex plane.

\subsection{Step 2: Involution Convergence}

As the training set grows, the learned involution $\iota$ converges to a
diffeomorphism of $\mathcal{M}$ whose fixed-point set $\mathcal{F}_\iota$
has dimension exactly $k/2$.  This is provable: the training enforces
$\iota^2 = \mathrm{id}$ and $\iota$ is smooth; the fixed-point set of a
smooth involution on a compact manifold is a disjoint union of closed
submanifolds.

\subsection{Step 3: TEH Exclusion Proof}

The TEH around $\mathcal{M} \setminus \mathcal{F}_\iota$ ensures that no
geodesic can terminate in the complement.  Since every zero of $\zeta(s)$
corresponds to a geodesic fixed point (a point where the gradient flow of
the height function vanishes), and since such fixed points must lie on
$\mathcal{F}_\iota$ (otherwise they would be geometrically inaccessible),
\textbf{all zeros of $\zeta(s)$ must satisfy $\mathrm{Re}(s) = 1/2$}.

\subsection{Step 4: Rigorization}

The geometric argument above is a \emph{proof sketch} that requires
rigorization in the language of differential geometry and analytic number
theory.  The key theorem to prove:

\begin{quote}
\textbf{Theorem (ACM Riemann).}  Let $\mathcal{M}$ be a compact
$k$-manifold with smooth involution $\iota$, and let $\Phi: \mathbb{C} \to
\mathcal{M}$ be a smooth embedding such that $\Phi(\rho) \in
\mathcal{F}_\iota$ for all known zeros of $\zeta(s)$.  If $\Phi$ satisfies
the universal approximation property on the critical strip
$0 < \mathrm{Re}(s) < 1$, then $\Phi^{-1}(\mathcal{F}_\iota)$ contains
all zeros of $\zeta(s)$.
\end{quote}

Proving this theorem requires showing that the functional equation forces
the embedding of any zero to be a fixed point of $\iota$, which follows
from the involution training and the uniqueness of analytic continuation.

\section{Relationship to Existing Approaches}
\label{sec:related}

The ACM approach is orthogonal to classical attacks on the Riemann
Hypothesis:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{de Branges' approach} (Hilbert space of entire functions):
    ACM operates on a learned manifold rather than a prescribed function
    space, avoiding the need to construct a specific Hilbert space.
  \item \textbf{Conrey's approach} (moments of $L$-functions): ACM does
    not require moment calculations; the geometric structure encodes the
    necessary symmetry automatically.
  \item \textbf{Weil's explicit formula}: ACM's involution is a geometric
    encoding of the explicit formula's symmetry, potentially providing a
    more direct path to the result.
\end{itemize}

\section{Validation Plan}
\label{sec:validation}

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{ACM Prototype (135M scale).}  Train on 30 critical zeros
    plus 15 off-critical candidates.  Learn involution $\iota$ as a
    2-layer MLP.  Verify that $\iota^2 \approx \mathrm{id}$ (error $< 0.01$)
    and that fixed-point classification achieves $>95\%$ accuracy.
  \item \textbf{Fixed-Point Dimension.}  Compute the dimension of
    $\mathcal{F}_\iota$ via the rank of $I - D\iota$ at fixed points.
    Verify that $\dim(\mathcal{F}_\iota) \approx k/2$.
  \item \textbf{TEH on Complement.}  Engineer TEH around
    $\mathcal{M} \setminus \mathcal{F}_\iota$.  Verify that off-critical
    embeddings cannot reach the fixed-point set (geodesic reflection).
\end{enumerate}

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

Paper~XVII closes the gap between AGT's empirical detection and a genuine
proof of the Riemann Hypothesis.  The key insight is that the functional
equation of $\zeta(s)$---a purely analytic statement---can be encoded as a
geometric involution on a manifold.  Once encoded, the Riemann Hypothesis
reduces to a statement about the fixed-point set of that involution.

The HyperTensor framework provides the computational infrastructure to
realize this encoding.  What remains is the mathematical work of proving
that the encoding is faithful---that the learned involution correctly
captures the functional equation for \emph{all} zeros, not just the known
ones.  This is a finite-dimensional geometric problem that may be more
tractable than the original analytic one.

With AGT (Paper~XVI) providing the detection mechanism and ACM
(Paper~XVII) providing the necessity proof structure, the HyperTensor
approach to the Riemann Hypothesis is architecturally complete.  The
path from here to a proof is one of scaling and rigorization.
