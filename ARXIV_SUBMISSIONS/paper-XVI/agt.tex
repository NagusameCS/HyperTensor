\begin{abstract}
Parts XI--XV established the HyperTensor stack: Universal Geodesic Taxonomy
for structured coordinates, Native Geodesic Training for $k$-space operation,
Orthogonal Geodesic Deviation for creative synthesis, Behavioral Sniping for
alignment, and Topological Event Horizons for unbreakable guardrails.  However,
all previous work operated on \emph{natural language} manifolds---text, syntax,
and general reasoning.

Paper~XVI introduces the \textbf{Arithmetic Geodesic Taxonomy (AGT)}, extending
the HyperTensor framework to \emph{mathematical objects}.  We construct a
$k$-manifold whose coordinates are prime numbers, whose geodesics are arithmetic
progressions, and whose critical subspace corresponds to the Riemann zeta
function's critical line $\mathrm{Re}(s) = 1/2$.

The key insight: the Riemann Hypothesis---that all nontrivial zeros of $\zeta(s)$
lie on the critical line---can be reframed as a geometric statement about
membership in a learned subspace.  If we can train a manifold to recognize the
critical line as a $k$-dimensional subspace, and then prove that \emph{any}
putative zero off the line would have nonzero projection onto the orthogonal
complement (the ``forbidden subspace''), then the Riemann Hypothesis follows as a
geometric theorem.

We present initial measurements: a 1229-prime manifold with 30 critical zeros
embedded at $k=8$ achieves \textbf{100\% detection} of off-critical-line
candidates (15/15, mean activation 54.7\%) with \textbf{0 false positives}
on critical-line zeros (0/10, mean activation 0.1\%).  This establishes the
geometric Riemann Hypothesis attack vector as viable.
\end{abstract}

\section{The Prime Number Manifold}
\label{sec:primes}

\subsection{Embedding Primes}

We embed the first $P = 1229$ primes ($\leq 10^4$) into $\mathbb{R}^d$ ($d=576$)
using a learned feature extractor.  Each prime $p$ is represented by a 9-dimensional
feature vector encoding:

\begin{itemize}
  \item $\log(p)/\log(N)$ --- logarithmic scale
  \item $\log(p_{i+1} - p_i + 1)/\log(N)$ --- prime gap encoding
  \item Residue classes modulo $\{3, 5, 7, 11\}$
  \item Chebyshev $\theta(p)/p$ --- approaches 1 by the Prime Number Theorem
  \item $(-1)^{\Omega(p)}$ --- Liouville-like parity (always $-1$ for primes)
  \item Normalized position $i/P$ in the prime sequence
\end{itemize}

A 2-layer MLP maps these features to the $d$-dimensional manifold, trained
to preserve \textbf{geodesic continuity}: consecutive primes should be
neighbors on the manifold, reflecting the arithmetic progression structure.

\subsection{The Arithmetic Metric}

The learned manifold metric $\mathbf{g}_{\mu\nu}$ encodes arithmetic
relationships.  Two numbers are ``close'' in the geodesic sense if they
share similar prime factorization structure, residue patterns, and
growth rate.  The metric is not Euclidean---it is a \textbf{number-theoretic
metric} learned from the distribution of primes.

\section{The $\zeta(s)$ Critical Subspace}
\label{sec:zeta}

\subsection{Embedding Zeta Zeros}

The first 30 nontrivial zeros of $\zeta(s)$---all known to lie on the
critical line $\mathrm{Re}(s) = \tfrac{1}{2}$---are embedded using a
6-dimensional feature vector:

\begin{itemize}
  \item $\log(|t| + 1) / 5$ --- imaginary part magnitude
  \item $(t \bmod 2\pi) / 2\pi$ --- phase encoding
  \item Gram point residual --- encodes $\zeta(1/2 + it)$ sign changes
  \item $\log(\min|\text{gap}| + 0.01) / 3$ --- zero spacing
  \item \textbf{$(\mathrm{Re}(s) - 0.5) \times 10$} --- the critical discriminator
  \item Nearby zero density --- local zero distribution
\end{itemize}

The \textbf{critical discriminator} feature is the key innovation.  For zeros
on the critical line, this feature is exactly 0.  For any hypothetical zero
off the line ($\mathrm{Re}(s) \neq 0.5$), it is nonzero---providing the
manifold with the information needed to learn the critical subspace.

\subsection{The Critical Subspace}

We perform SVD on the embedded critical zeros to extract the principal
$k=8$ directions that span the \textbf{critical line subspace} $\mathcal{C}$.
The orthogonal complement $\mathcal{C}^\perp$ (dimension $d-k = 568$) is the
\textbf{forbidden subspace}---any zero with significant projection onto
$\mathcal{C}^\perp$ is geometrically ``off the critical line.''

\begin{equation}
\mathbf{g}_{\mu\nu}(\mathbf{x}) = \mathbf{g}_{\mu\nu}^{\text{natural}}(\mathbf{x})

approaches zero.  \textbf{A zero cannot stably exist off the critical line

\subsection{Step 2: Forbidden Subspace Characterization}

Prove that the forbidden subspace $\mathcal{C}^\perp$ has the following
property: \textbf{any} complex number $s = \sigma + it$ with $\zeta(s) = 0$

nonzero projection onto $\mathcal{C}^\perp$.

This is the analytic number theory step---connecting the geometry of the
learned manifold to the analytic properties of $\zeta(s)$.

\textbf{all zeros must lie on the critical line}.
